Sort a strategic thread into a KPI cascade

A budget can be accurate and still be useless to leadership, if nothing in it traces back to what the strategy actually asks for. A KPI cascade is how an analyst fixes that: a corporate goal at the top, the financial ratios that would confirm it in the middle, and the day-to-day measures a business unit controls at the bottom. Below is one real strategic thread. Sort it into its three tiers, and set aside anything that doesn't actually belong.

FP&A stands for financial planning and analysis. A cascade only works when every measure traces up to a goal and down to an action a team can take. A few terms before you start.

Corporate objective
The strategic goal itself, stated in the plan's own words, not in numbers.
Financial target
The accounting-term ratio or figure that would confirm the objective is being met.
Operational KPI
The specific, day-to-day measure a business unit actually controls.
Set aside
Tracked somewhere, maybe, but not something that confirms or drives this particular objective.

            Trace it both ways. Days-to-close per claim and adjuster productivity are both Claims-owned measures, and both feed the loss-adjustment-expense ratio — the cost of investigating and settling claims. Underwriting consistency is Underwriting's measure, and it feeds the expense ratio — the cost of writing and servicing policies. Both ratios roll up into the one corporate objective. The number of claims received tells you volume, not performance: it doesn't show whether cycle time is improving or margin is holding, so it never earns a place in this particular cascade.
