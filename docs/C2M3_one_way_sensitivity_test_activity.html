Sensitivity analysis

Find the driver that moves the plan most

A driver model is not finished when the formulas work. It is finished when you know which assumption to worry about. Two plans can be equally "done" and yet one is fragile, because a small miss on a single driver swings the result while the others barely matter. A one-way sensitivity test finds that load-bearing driver, so you know where to spend your estimating care and your controls.

FP&A stands for financial planning and analysis — the team that builds a company's plan and answers "what if?" A driver is an operational number a planner sets, such as volume or price; the model then calculates the financial lines from it. A one-way sensitivity test raises one driver by a fixed amount, holds the others still, and records how far a chosen result moves. Run it for every driver and the drivers rank themselves, largest mover to smallest. A few terms before you start.

Operating profit
What is left after every operating cost is paid — the bottom line this test watches.
Take rate
The share of each dollar of payment volume the business keeps as revenue.
Processing cost
Cost of goods sold (COGS): the network and handling cost of running each payment.
Loss provision
Money set aside for expected fraud and chargebacks on the volume processed.
Operating expense
Fixed running costs — salaries, systems, rent — that do not rise with a day's volume.

Raising volume 10% does not simply add 10% of revenue. More payments earn 10% more revenue, but they also cost 10% more to process and carry 10% more loss. Only what is left — the gross-profit margin on that extra volume — reaches operating profit. Operating expense stays put.

Raise payment volume by 10%, hold the other drivers still. By how many dollars does operating profit change?

Revenue rises $4,326,679, but processing cost rises $1,276,917 and loss rises $77,005 with it. Operating expense is unchanged. Net to operating profit: $4,326,679 − $1,276,917 − $77,005 = $2,972,757.

The lesson lives in that subtraction. Because volume drags cost and loss up alongside revenue, a 10% volume rise moves operating profit by 10% of gross profit, not 10% of revenue. A driver that moved only revenue — the take rate — would move profit more for the same 10%. That is the kind of surprise a one-way test is built to surface. See it for every driver at once below.
