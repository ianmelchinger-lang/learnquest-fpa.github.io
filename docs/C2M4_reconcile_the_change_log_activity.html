Change-log discipline

Reconcile a change log

When a budget review agrees to change something, the change has to be written down — who asked, which driver moved, why, and by how many dollars. That last field is the one people skip, and skipping it is expensive. A change log you can trust is one where the dollar impacts of every agreed change add up to the amount the plan actually moved. If they do not add up, a change slipped through unrecorded, and no one can trace where the money went.

FP&A stands for financial planning and analysis — the team that builds a company's plan. A driver is an operating number a planner sets, such as volume or price; the plan calculates the financial lines from it. A change log is the running record of every agreed change to the plan. The impact of a change is the dollar amount it adds to or takes from the bottom line. To reconcile the log is to confirm the impacts sum to the change in the bottom line — the parts adding up to the whole. A few terms before you start.

Operating profit
What is left after every operating cost is paid — the bottom line this log tracks.
Take rate
The share of each dollar of payment volume the business keeps as revenue; raising it raises revenue.
Cost rate
Processing cost (COGS) per dollar of volume; lowering it lowers cost.
Loss rate
Money set aside for fraud and chargebacks per dollar of volume.
Operating expense
Fixed running costs — salaries, systems, rent; a change here is a flat dollar amount.

Route 1 — from the change itself

The take rate lifts one line: revenue. So the impact is 8% of revenue.
Revenue × 8%  =  $43,266,787 × 0.08  =  +$3,461,343

Route 2 — from the reconciliation

The three recorded rows come to +$638,459 + $77,005 − $1,200,000 = −$484,537.
The plan moved +$2,976,806 in all, so the blank must be $2,976,806 − (−$484,537) = +$3,461,343.

The two routes agree, and that agreement is the point. One route comes from the driver, the other from making the column add up; when they land on the same number, the log reconciles and every dollar of the plan's move is accounted for. A blank row, or one with the wrong sign, breaks that trace — the plan would show a move that the log cannot explain. Discipline here is not bookkeeping neatness; it is what lets each person see their change reflected and follow it to the result.

Move the changes and watch the log reconcile

No right or wrong here. Set the size of each agreed change and its impact recomputes. Every change is applied to the plan; the checkbox decides whether it is also written into the log. Keep every applied change logged and the two totals match — the log reconciles. Leave one out of the log and watch a gap open: the plan moved, but the log cannot say why. Volume stays fixed throughout, which is what keeps the impacts adding up cleanly.
