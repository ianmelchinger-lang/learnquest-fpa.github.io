The FP&A monthly cycle

Put the monthly FP&A cycle in order

Every month, an FP&A team runs the same short loop: take what already happened, work out what changed and why, turn that into an updated view of what's ahead, and hand it to the person who has to decide. The stages below are the loop for one month, but they are out of order. Your job is to put them in the order an analyst actually works them.

FP&A stands for financial planning and analysis. Unlike accounting, which records what already happened, FP&A looks forward: it uses the record to say what is coming and what to do about it. A few plain-language terms before you start.

Actuals
What actually happened last month, the recorded results.
The close
When the controller (the person who owns the accounting record) shuts the books for the month, so the actuals are final and correct.
Budget
The approved plan for the year, the fixed target you measure against.
Variance
The gap between the plan and the actuals: what moved, and by how much.
Forecast
The live projection of where the business is now heading, updated as things change. Not the same as the fixed budget.
Business partner
The operating leader who runs a part of the business and has a decision to make, here the head of the claims unit.

Order the four stages, then check

Last month, the claims unit's loss-adjustment costs ran over plan, and the claims leader wants to know what happened and what to do. Use the Up and Down buttons to arrange the four stages of your monthly cycle from first to last. The number on the left shows each stage's current position. When you think the order is right, check it.

    The order an analyst works

    1. Confirm the close. Wait for the controller to finish the close, then take the final actuals. Everything after this is built on them.
    2. Analyze the variance. Compare the actuals to the plan, find the lines that moved, and work out why, here, why loss-adjustment ran over.
    3. Update the forecast. Use what the variance showed to reshape the forward view of where the unit is now heading.
    4. Package it for the business partner. Turn it into a short recommendation in the claims leader's own terms, while the decision is still open.
    The order is not a matter of taste. Each stage feeds the next: you need final actuals before you can measure a variance, you need the variance before you can update the forecast honestly, and you need the updated forecast before there is anything worth handing over. The last stage, packaging, is the one that turns careful analysis into a decision instead of a file.

    See where the loop breaks

Switch any stage off and watch the chain break at that point, with a plain note on what goes wrong. Turn stages back on to close the loop. The lesson is simple: skip any one stage and a decision never reaches the claims leader.
